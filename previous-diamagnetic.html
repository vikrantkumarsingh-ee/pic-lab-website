Six-Degree-of-Freedom Diamagnetically Levitated Nanopositioners

Compact passive levitation with symmetric dual-sided electromagnetic actuation.

Doctoral research · IISc Bangalore · Completed and published

Project overview

This project developed compact positioning stages that combine passive diamagnetic levitation with planar electromagnetic actuation. A symmetric dual-sided architecture was introduced to overcome force coupling, limited stiffness, low payload capacity, and vibration sensitivity associated with conventional single-sided actuation.

Multi-zone and compliant-stage configurations subsequently enabled large-range motion along all six degrees of freedom. The stages achieved 5 mm linear travel with positioning precision better than 1.88 nm, approximately 1.1 rad angular travel with 50 µrad resolution, and compact volumes of approximately 10–20 cm³.

Why the work mattered

In a single-sided system, increasing lateral trapping current also increases downward electromagnetic force. This reduces levitation height and eventually causes contact with the diamagnetic substrate. The dual-sided architecture cancels undesirable out-of-plane force while reinforcing useful in-plane forces and stiffness.

Technical approach

  • Passive diamagnetic levitation
  • Symmetric upper and lower PCB actuation
  • Multi-zone force and torque generation
  • Current-based force, torque, and stiffness allocation
  • Top- and side-view optical measurement
  • Subpixel digital image correlation and real-time multi-axis commands

Research leadership and contribution

Dr. Singh led the experimental and system-level development of the diamagnetically levitated nanopositioners. Building on Prof. G. R. Jayanth’s initial research direction, he identified the force-coupling and vibration limitations of single-sided actuation and proposed the symmetric dual-PCB architecture used to overcome them. He performed the initial electromagnetic simulations, developed and integrated the positioning stages, implemented the real-time multi-axis actuation and image-based measurement systems, and conducted the complete experimental characterization and AFM tip-replacement demonstration. The analytical framework and manuscript were developed jointly with Prof. Jayanth, with Ms. Pranali Gaydhane contributing to the subpixel position-measurement code.

Connection to current PIC Lab research

This work established foundations in passive levitation, compact six-axis actuation, current-based load allocation, machine-vision metrology, and instrument-integrated precision control. It directly motivates the current Contactless Multi-Axis Precision Positioning program.
