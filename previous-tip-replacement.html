Automated In-Situ AFM Tip Replacement

Vision-guided micro-assembly for rapid replacement of worn and fractured AFM tips.

Doctoral research · IISc Bangalore · Completed and published

Project overview

This project developed a vision-guided system that replaces only the worn or damaged tip of an AFM probe rather than discarding the complete cantilever. A phase-change wax microgripper, focused laser heating, hybrid coarse–fine positioning, and automated visual feedback were integrated into a complete tip-exchange instrument.

Why the work mattered

AFM tips become worn, contaminated, or fractured through repeated use and high-force interactions. Replacing only the tip can reduce downtime and cost, retain specialized active cantilevers, avoid unnecessary recalibration, and enable different tip geometries to be exchanged on demand.

Technical approach

  • Phase-change paraffin-wax microgripping
  • Focused laser heating
  • Microfabricated tip-supply and tip-holder stations
  • Motorized coarse and piezoelectric fine positioning
  • Image-correlation-based in-plane measurement
  • Image-blur-based out-of-plane measurement
  • Automated docking, pickup, and detachment
  • Contact- and tapping-mode AFM validation

Selected results

10 sAverage automated tip-replacement time.
<0.3 µmPositioning accuracy and repeatability.
RestoredImaging after progressive wear and nanoindentation fracture.

Research leadership and contribution

Dr. Singh led the system-level design, automation, software development, integration, and experimental validation of the AFM tip-replacement platform. Building on Prof. G. R. Jayanth’s original wax-microgripper concept, he conceived the focused-laser phase-control and vision-guided automation strategies, developed the coarse–fine positioning and control system, and performed the experiments demonstrating rapid and repeatable tip exchange. Wax microsphere preparation was contributed by K. Hithiksha.

Connection to current PIC Lab research

This project established expertise in automated micro-assembly, visual-feedback control, coarse–fine positioning, optical phase control, and complete scientific-instrument integration. These capabilities support the current Parallel Atomic Force Microscopy program, where scalable operation will require probe alignment, calibration, maintenance, and replacement.
